01

2-D Compressible Flashing & Cavitating Flow Solver (IAPWS-95)

+
loc University of Cambridge, UK dates 2025 – Present role Sole developer · C++17

Project Overview

An edge-based finite-volume solver for 2-D viscous compressible flow of water and steam, built from scratch in C++ to study flash boiling and cavitation in nozzles. It couples a full real-fluid equation of state with finite-rate phase-change physics and is validated against the Abuaf BNL-309 flashing-nozzle experiment.

Numerical Methods

  • Edge-based Roe approximate Riemann solver on a median-dual (vertex-centred) control volume
  • 3-tier reconstruction — WENO5 → MUSCL+minmod → 1st-order near boundaries — with JST artificial dissipation
  • Explicit Jameson 4-stage Runge–Kutta with local time-stepping; optional BDF1/2 dual-time for unsteady runs
  • Planar and axisymmetric geometry; characteristic inlet/outlet, wall, and axis boundary conditions
  • Optional Spalart–Allmaras turbulence model; OpenMP-parallel hot loops

Physical Modeling

  • Full IAPWS-95 (Helmholtz free-energy) equation of state for water/steam across all phases, with a tabulated cache for speed
  • Homogeneous Equilibrium (HEM) and Homogeneous Relaxation (HRM) phase-change models — transporting vapour mass fraction and relaxing it toward equilibrium over the Downar–Zapolski timescale
  • Physics-based interfacial heat-transfer source term driving non-equilibrium evaporation

Validation & Results

Against the BNL-309 converging–diverging nozzle (subcooled water flashing to two-phase), both planar and half-axisymmetric cases flash with void onset at the throat. On the rising limb the half-axisymmetric result tracks the experimental void-fraction data closely. Verified with unit tests for the EOS (17/17) and HRM closure (26/26).

Stack

  • C++17, CMake, OpenMP for the solver core
  • Real-fluid thermodynamics (IAPWS-95), Riemann solvers, finite-rate phase change
  • Python for case generation, post-processing, and publication-grade contour/validation plots

Adaptable Acoustic Panel for Dynamic Sound Environments

+
loc Cooper Union, NY dates Fall 2024 – Spring 2025 team 3 ME + 1 Architecture

Project Overview

Developed a low-cost, tunable acoustic panel to solve a common problem in multipurpose venues: balancing clarity and ambience. The panel combines fiberglass absorption with switchable diffusion to adapt to different acoustic needs — from orchestral performances to spoken word. A custom mechanism lets users rotate between two frequency-specific diffuser profiles.

Engineering Highlights

  • Designed and prototyped a binary amplitude diffuser with rotating wooden slats and chain-driven roller assemblies
  • Tested in real-world acoustic environments using RT60 and ISO 17497-2 standards
  • Reduced reverberation by up to 60% in mid frequencies; achieved diffusion coefficients near 0.7 — on par with commercial panels
  • Validated Python-based acoustic models (Sabine, Eyring, Fitzroy) against experimental data
  • Fabricated components using laser cutting, CNC machining, and hand assembly

Skills Used

  • Acoustic engineering & ISO testing
  • CAD (SolidWorks), prototyping, and fabrication
  • Python for simulation & data analysis
  • Collaboration with mechanical & architectural designers

Impact

This project reflects a year of hands-on lab experience applying engineering principles to real-world testing and product design, bridging architectural form with mechanical function for dynamic sound control in public spaces.

Tracking Strain in Stress Concentration Using Digital Image Correlation

+
loc Cooper Union, NY dates Spring 2024 role ME Researcher

Project Overview

Experimental stress analysis using Digital Image Correlation (DIC) to validate theoretical stress-concentration data. The goal was to track strain distribution around concentration points — such as holes in engineering components — using high-resolution imaging and DIC software.

Skills Used

  • SolidWorks for CAD and FEM analysis
  • MATLAB for data processing and contour plotting
  • Digital Image Correlation (DIC) techniques
  • Instron testing for tensile loading
  • Experimental setup and data collection with VIC-3D

Description

Used DIC to analyze strain distribution around stress-concentration areas in aluminum samples. Specimens were loaded in a tensile machine while observed with a high-resolution stereo camera, then strain data was compared with theoretical and simulation results to identify deviations and refine procedures.

Key Contributions

  • Tested aluminum 6061 samples with applied speckle patterns for DIC
  • Used an Instron tensile machine for loading while recording via VIC-3D
  • Processed strain data in MATLAB into detailed stress-concentration contour plots
  • Compared experimental results against theoretical models and FEM simulations
