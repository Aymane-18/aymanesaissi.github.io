Adaptable Acoustic Panel for Dynamic Sound Environments

Location: Cooper Union, New York, NY

Duration: Fall 2024 – Spring 2025

Team: 3 Mechanical Engineers + 1 Architecture Collaborator

Project Overview

Developed a low-cost, tunable acoustic panel to solve a common problem in multipurpose venues: balancing clarity and ambience. The panel combines fiberglass absorption with switchable diffusion to adapt to different acoustic needs—from orchestral performances to spoken word. A custom-built mechanism enables users to rotate between two frequency-specific diffuser profiles.

Engineering Highlights

  • Designed and prototyped a binary amplitude diffuser with rotating wooden slats and chain-driven roller assemblies
  • Tested in real-world acoustic environments using RT60 and ISO 17497-2 standards
  • Reduced reverberation by up to 60% in mid frequencies; achieved diffusion coefficients near 0.7—on par with commercial panels
  • Validated Python-based acoustic models (Sabine, Eyring, Hybrid) against experimental data
  • Fabricated components using laser cutting, CNC machining, and hand assembly in a hardware lab

Skills Used

  • Acoustic Engineering & ISO Testing
  • CAD (SolidWorks), Prototyping, and Fabrication
  • Python Programming for Simulation & Data Analysis
  • Team Collaboration with Mechanical & Architectural Designers

Impact

This project showcases one year of hands-on lab experience applying engineering principles to real-world testing and product design. It bridges architectural form with mechanical function, offering a scalable solution for dynamic sound control in public spaces.

Skills Used

Simulation Results

Open Channel Flow & Nozzle Optimization – Published ASME Research

Location: Cooper Union, New York, NY

Duration: Fall 2022 – Spring 2025

Role: Lab Researcher & CFD Contributor (Published Author)

Project Overview

Conducted a year-long research project on open channel flows and nozzle optimization, culminating in the co-authored paper “Workshops for Active Learning and the Draining Tank”, accepted to the ASME 2025 International Mechanical Engineering Congress & Exposition (IMECE2025). This work integrated theory, lab experimentation, and CFD simulation to analyze transient draining tank behavior and flow losses through nozzle contractions.

Engineering Contributions

  • Developed custom nozzles for experimental testing and fabricated them in the machine shop
  • Conducted independent CFD simulations to analyze pressure, velocity, and loss coefficients across Reynolds regimes
  • Validated experimental results against Torricelli Law, Bernoulli principles, and entropy-based flow models
  • Led parametric studies on loss coefficients for varying geometries and flow rates using Python and ANSYS Fluent
  • Collaborated with professors and Air Force Academy researchers to integrate multi-modal teaching approaches (lecture, workshop, lab)

Skills Used

  • CFD (ANSYS Fluent), SolidWorks, Python for flow analysis and visualization
  • Experimental design, high-speed imaging, pressure and velocity measurements
  • Thermal-fluid modeling: Torricelli Law, Reynolds Transport Theorem, entropy balance
  • Scientific writing and technical presentation (ASME standards)

Media & Simulation Results

Impact

This project not only supported curriculum redesign at Cooper Union using active-learning workshops but also gave me publication experience at a major engineering conference. My contribution to the CFD simulations and nozzle design served as an independent graduate-level study on complex fluid interactions in constrained geometries.

Autonomous Mobile Robot Research Project

Location: Cooper Union, New York, NY

Duration: Fall 2024

Project Overview

This project involves the design and development of an autonomous mobile robot intended for competitive robotics applications. The robot is equipped with sensors and actuators to navigate a dynamic environment, avoid obstacles, and complete specific tasks such as object detection and ball launching. The goal is to optimize the robot's performance in mobility, accuracy, and reliability under various operational conditions.

Skills Used

  • CAD Design with SolidWorks and Autodesk
  • Arduino programming for robotics control
  • Sensor integration (Ultrasonic, IR sensors)
  • 3D printing for custom components
  • Motor control with L298N motor driver
  • System integration for efficient communication between sensors and actuators

System Design and Media

Project Description

The autonomous robot is designed with a clear acrylic frame and is powered by 12V brushed DC motors. The robot features an array of ultrasonic and infrared sensors for obstacle detection and environmental awareness. The central task of the robot is to launch ping-pong balls using a spring plunger mechanism, activated by a solenoid. A custom 3D-printed barrel is used to launch the balls with precision. The robot is controlled by an Arduino UNO, which processes sensor data and sends commands to the motors and solenoid for action.

Key Contributions

  • Designed the robot frame using SolidWorks and fabricated components using laser cutting and 3D printing techniques.
  • Developed the drive system utilizing custom motor mounts and high-traction wheels for stable movement.
  • Integrated a sensor array, consisting of ultrasonic and IR sensors, to enable obstacle avoidance and target detection.
  • Programmed the Arduino to handle sensor input, control the drive motors, and trigger the ping-pong ball launcher.
  • Created a sensor mount system to reduce external clutter and ensure reliable sensor readings.
  • Tested the robot's functionality in various scenarios to ensure robustness and reliability in the field.

Tracking Strain in Stress Concentration Using Digital Image Correlation

Location: Cooper Union, New York, NY

Duration: Spring 2024

Role: Mechanical Engineering Researcher

Project Overview

This project focuses on experimental stress analysis using Digital Image Correlation (DIC) to validate theoretical stress concentration data. It aims to track strain distribution around stress concentration points, such as holes in engineering components, using high-resolution imaging and DIC software.

Skills Used

  • SolidWorks for CAD and FEM analysis
  • MATLAB for data processing and contour plotting
  • Digital Image Correlation (DIC) techniques
  • Instron testing for tensile loading
  • Experimental setup and data collection using VIC-3D software

Project Description

In this project, the team used DIC technology to analyze the strain distribution around stress concentration areas in aluminum samples. The samples were tested in a tensile machine while being observed with a high-resolution stereo camera. The strain data was compared with theoretical and simulation results to identify deviations and optimize experimental procedures.

Media and Results

Key Contributions

  • Conducted experimental tests using aluminum 6061 samples and applied speckle patterns for DIC.
  • Used Instron tensile machine for sample loading while recording strain data through VIC-3D software.
  • Processed strain data in MATLAB to create detailed contour plots of stress concentration.
  • Compared experimental results with theoretical models and FEM simulations to validate findings.
